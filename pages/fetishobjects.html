◢ AIM

◢ In 1969, when the US was leading the third world in a conspiracy of destructive violence, AIM was born in New York City's East Village

◢ AIM was a confluence of radical ideologies: anarchism, left wing, communist, Maoist, and anti-globalisation, along with national liberation, feminism, ecology, animal rights, black power, and LGBTQ liberation.

◢ It also had strong Marxist undercurrents. We were united in our desire to radically transform the US government and economy.

◢ US wild-child hardcore punk bands like Black Flag and the Minutemen and politicised youngsters who had become disillusioned with US capitalism helped AIM expand and attract international followers.

◢ AIM co-founder John Sinclair wrote in his autobiography, Black Flag Through The Eyes of a Young Prisoner of Capitalism, that they were against the military-industrial complex, and the suffering caused by the Vietnam War.

◢ The FBI infiltrated and pressured AIM to arrest all the current and past members, or at least keep them under surveillance.

◢ But AIM members such as Eric McDavid, who took the stand as a witness at the trial of the MOVE bombing on September 13, 1985, for refusing to give up arms in the name of a corrupt government, would defiantly refuse to back down.

◢ Eric's hands were cut by a buzz saw, but he refused to submit to authority.

◢ In the end, Eric was freed from jail on bail, but he was sentenced to 18 years to life in prison, served 11 years, and was released on parole.

◢ Eric's activism and relentless fire for social justice won him international acclaim, not just for AIM but also for the People's Temple, a missionary organisation of which he was a leader.

◢ Eric died on October 14, 1997.
